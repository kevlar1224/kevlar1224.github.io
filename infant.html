Infant Mortality Rates

Infant Mortality Rates by Race

The data on infant mortality rates in California was collected from data.gov and aggregated by user @health on data.world. (here). The data was collected from between 2004 and 2013.

As with the previous two factors, Black Americans experienced the highest rate. However, while people of Mixed Race were somewhat middling in terms poverty and HIV/AIDS infection rates, they had the second highest infant mortality rate, with over twice the overall rate (4.95 per 1000 live births).

A study by the Journal of Women's Health found that children of two black parents and children of one black parent and one white parent had significantly higher rates of infant mortality, which corresponds to the data here. The higher mortality rates were attributed to higher rates of low birth weights and premature birth, which according to an article by National Institutes of Health are frequent causes of infant mortality (the second most common in 2011).

Infant Mortality Count by Race

Again, although Black American and Mixed Race infants had the highest rates mortality, they both make up small portions of the population. Hispanic infants, despite experiencing roughly average rates of mortality, (5.07 per 1000 live births compared to 4.95), accounted for more than half of all infant deaths in California for the time period.

Infant Mortality Rates by Sex

The dataset also accounts for the age of the deceased infants - categorized as either neonatal (newborn) or postneonatal (surviving past the first four weeks) as well as their sex. Neonatal infants were far more likely to die than postneonatal infants. This can likely be explained by premature births - which the article by National Institues of Health states is a leading cause of infant mortality - affecting infants early on, while infants that reached the postneonatal stage would likely have outlived any negative effects.

Male infants had a noticeably, though not significantly, higher rate of mortality. This could be attributed to higher susceptibility to various birth or developmental defects.
